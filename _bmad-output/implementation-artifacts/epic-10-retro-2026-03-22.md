## Successes & Strengths

1. **Process investments compounded.** Correction budget, investigation-first spikes, and vertical-slice minimum all paid real dividends. Epic 10 is the first epic where prior retro investments compounded instead of just surviving contact with reality. The correction budget wasn't just a number — it shaped how we reacted when 10-0-2a and 10-0-2b emerged. No emergency replanning, no scope panic.

2. **Outcome direction validation (10-0-2b) caught a catastrophic flaw.** A 4-layer failure chain — Polymarket catalog blindly taking `clobTokenIds[0]`, Kalshi returning no outcome metadata, TF-IDF ranking both sides equally, LLM scoring using soft guidance — produced phantom edges of 27-30% and APR 1500%+ for UFC fights. These would have been unhedged directional bets. Caught during a pre-epic debt story instead of in production. Constraints over vigilance in action.

When the five-criteria model became six during 10.2, the team absorbed it as discovery — not rework — because the design spike had already mapped the space. 4. **Unified sizing formula (10.4) eliminated capital over-deployment.** Discovered the existing independent per-leg formula was deploying up to 20.96% over budget. Fix: `idealCount = floor(reservedCapitalUsd / (primaryDivisor + secondaryDivisor))`. Simpler and correct.

5. **316 tests, zero scope explosions, zero production incidents.** ATDD approach provided scaffolding even when pre-generated tests needed fixes. Correction budget held at 29%. Every story completed, none dropped.

## Challenges & Growth Areas

### Three Recurring Defect Classes

All three share one shape: **silent correctness failures that pass all existing tests.** Detected by review, not by automated enforcement.

| Defect Class | Recurrence | Why It's Invisible | What's Missing |
|---|---|---|---|
| Event wiring gaps (`@OnEvent` missing, dead handlers, unregistered subscribers) | 4/9 stories (44%) | No error thrown, unit tests pass, handler logic correct in isolation | Nothing verifies emitter→subscriber connection exists |
| Unbounded collection leaks (Maps/Sets without cleanup) | 3/9 stories (33%) | Feature works, tests pass, leak only manifests under sustained runtime | Cleanup not in ACs, no workflow step asks "what cleans this up?" |
| Paper/live mode contamination (`isPaper` filter omissions) | 2/9 stories (22%) | Tests run in single mode, cross-mode bleeding only visible in mixed state | No cross-mode test fixtures, `isPaper` filter not enforced structurally |

### Story 10-0-1 Was Oversized

Seven phases, 17 tasks, 77+ tests — and 5 CRITICAL findings in review. The story was inherently complex (abstracting over two fundamentally different WebSocket protocols), but the scope made Agreement #19 (internal subsystem verification) impractical. Integration seams between phases were missed because the developer's mental model fragmented at that scale. The story could have been split into "subscription establishment" and "divergence monitoring" as independently deployable stories.

**Lesson:** Story size is a leading indicator for integration risk. Agreement #19 difficulty scales with scope.

### Epic 9 Action Item #3 Not Addressed

The recurring MEDIUM prevention analysis — committed to in the Epic 9 retro — was not executed. Same MEDIUM categories (incomplete test assertions, dead code, missing DTO validation) recurred across stories, caught by review every time, never prevented structurally.

**Lesson:** Open-ended discipline commitments without enforcement mechanisms don't get done. Not because anyone's lazy — because our process is optimized for stories and has no mechanism for tracking cross-cutting disciplines.

### Post-Deploy Bug in 10.1

Raw SQL `SELECT COUNT(*) FROM open_positions WHERE status IN (...)` did NOT filter by `is_paper`, so 3 paper positions triggered a LIVE halt. Additionally, `recalculateRiskBudget()` inside `reconcile()` re-persisted stale halt reasons. Caught within hours, fixed, but preventable if the paper/live boundary test suite (Action Item #5) had been completed.

### ATDD Test Quality

Pre-generated acceptance tests in Stories 10.2 and 10.4 consistently needed significant fixes — wrong mock targets, wrong field names, wrong method calls, incorrect assertions. The scaffold is valuable (40+ tests to fix is faster than 40 tests from scratch), but execution needs refinement.

## Key Insights & Lessons Learned

1. **Disciplines drift, deliverables ship.** Retro commitments must be expressible as stories with acceptance criteria to survive. Our process is optimized for stories — feed disciplines through the story machine instead of bolting them on from outside. (Drives Agreement #24)

2. **Story size is a leading indicator for integration risk.** When a story gets large enough that the developer's mental model fragments, integration seams between phases get missed. Subsystem verification (Agreement #19) becomes harder to apply as scope increases. (Drives Agreement #25)

3. **Three recurring defect classes need structural guards, not vigilance.** Event wiring, collection lifecycle, and mode contamination all share the "silent correctness failure" shape. Review caught them every time — but relying on review as the last line of defense for 44% of stories is the "vigilance over constraints" trap. (Drives Agreement #26)

4. **Constraints over vigilance applies to process too.** The same principle that gave us `decimal.js` for financial math and Zod for API validation should apply to retro commitments. Encode disciplines as enforceable constraints — test templates, linter rules, checklist items — not aspirational agreements.

5. **The half-life of problems is the best health indicator.** Event wiring gaps existed for 4 stories before being named. The isPaper inventory was carried for 2 retros. The MEDIUM analysis lasted 1 retro as an orphan. The team is getting faster at converting observations into structural responses. That trend matters more than any individual metric.

6. **Epic 10 proved capability; Epic 11 must prove sustainability.** The system now autonomously evaluates six exit criteria, adapts leg sequencing, manages single-leg exposure, and continuously recalculates edge. The structural guards defined in this retro aren't process niceties — they're prerequisites for live deployment. The gap between paper trading and live deployment is closing.

## Team Agreements

### New Agreements (Epic 10 Retro)

- **#24: Retro commitments as deliverables.** Every retro action item must be expressible as a story with ACs or a task within a story. If it can't be, rephrase it until it can. "Discipline" commitments that depend on sustained attention without enforcement mechanism are rejected at retro time.

- **#25: Story size as integration risk indicator.** Stories exceeding 10 tasks or 3+ integration boundaries are flagged for splitting. Pre-split review during story preparation, not after implementation reveals the problem.

- **#26: Structural guards over review vigilance.** Recurring defect classes get structural prevention (test templates, linter rules, startup checks), not "be more careful" agreements. If review catches the same category three times, it becomes a pre-epic story.

### Carried Forward (23 prior agreements remain in effect)

All 23 prior agreements carried forward. Agreements #18 (vertical slice), #19 (subsystem verification), #20 (paper/live boundary), #22 (correction budget) validated in Epic 10. Agreements #19 and #20 now reinforced by structural enforcement via Stories 11-0-1 and 11-0-2.
